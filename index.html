Masked by Consensus: Disentangling Privileged Knowledge in LLM Correctness

Tomer Ashuach, Liat Ein-Dor, Shai Gretz, Yoav Katz, Yonatan Belinkov
Technion – Israel Institute of Technology, IBM Research
Paper arXiv

Abstract

Humans use introspection to evaluate their understanding through private internal states inaccessible to external observers. Recent work suggests that large language models possess similar privileged knowledge—information unavailable through external observation. In this work, we ask whether models have unique signals about answer correctness given only the question. We train correctness classifiers on question representations from both a model's own hidden states and external models, finding no advantage: self-probes perform comparably to peer-model probes. We hypothesize this is due to high inter-model agreement of question-answer correctness. To isolate genuine privileged knowledge, we construct disagreement subsets, where models produce conflicting predictions. Here, we discover domain-specific privileged knowledge: self-representations consistently outperform peer representations in factual knowledge tasks, but show no advantage in mathematical reasoning. We explore potential mechanisms underlying this distinction, finding evidence that factual correctness relies on entity-driven memory retrieval while mathematical correctness may involve more universal computational patterns.

Method Overview

We train correctness probes on a model's own hidden states (self-probe) and on external models' representations (external-probe), then measure the Premium Gap: the advantage of self over external. We evaluate across three model families (Gemma-2-9B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B) and five datasets spanning factual knowledge and mathematical reasoning.

The Surprise: No Premium Gap

On full test sets, self-probes offer no advantage over external probes. Cross-model probes match or exceed self-probe performance across most settings, suggesting—at first glance—that LLMs have no privileged knowledge about their own correctness.

Why? The Agreement Confound

Models agree on correctness 77–83% of the time. This high consensus lets external probes use their own model's correctness as a proxy, masking any genuine privileged signal. To break this confound, we evaluate on disagreement subsets—questions where models give conflicting answers—eliminating the external proxy.

Privileged Knowledge Re-emerges

On disagreement subsets, a statistically significant premium gap (~5%) re-emerges in factual tasks in 8/9 configs (linear) and 9/9 (MLP). Self-probes outperform all external probes on disagreement subsets, proving that models retain unique internal signals inaccessible to external observers. In contrast, mathematical reasoning shows no premium gap even under disagreement: correctness is determined by universal difficulty patterns accessible to any model.

Why the Domain Difference?

Factual Is Entity-Driven Memory Retrieval

We hypothesize that factual correctness depends on model-specific memory traces. Lexical-only probes (using just entities and nouns) recover up to 75% of probe performance, suggesting the correctness signal stems largely from concept familiarity. The idiosyncratic presence of these memories—unique to each model—may be the source of privileged knowledge in factual tasks.

Math Universal Computational Patterns

Mathematical reasoning, however, appears to follow difficulty patterns observable to any capable model. External predictors derived from peer consensus perform strongly, supporting this hypothesis. While lexical tokens in advanced math (e.g., "eigenvalue") hold some signal, we speculate they merely encode broader topics. These universally observable patterns might explain the absence of privileged knowledge, though further investigation is needed.

Conclusion

LLMs do possess privileged knowledge about their own correctness—but only in factual domains. Standard evaluation masks this: high inter-model agreement lets external probes exploit peer correctness as a proxy, making the premium gap vanish. Evaluating on disagreement subsets removes this confound and reveals a clear domain split: a significant self-probe advantage in factual tasks, none in mathematical reasoning, pointing to model-specific memory traces as the source of privileged knowledge.
